Search engines have significant influence over what information people see, so their design should be transparent and should not secretly favor advertisers, political interests, or particular organizations. Paid results should be clearly labeled and separated from ordinary search results. Users should also be told, in understandable terms, which factors affect ranking, such as relevance to the query, reliability, recency, and popularity.

A search engine should try to return relevant and high-quality information while also presenting more than one credible perspective when a topic is disputed. Personalization can be useful, but it should not trap users in a narrow “filter bubble.” Users should be able to turn personalization off, inspect or change the information used for personalization, and understand why a result was recommended.

The system should be tested for systematic bias, misinformation, and unfair exclusion of smaller or less powerful sources. Ranking rules and sponsored content should be audited regularly. In a simple project such as Bajillion, the ranking rule is transparent: a file is returned only when it contains every term in the query. A larger search engine would need additional quality and fairness checks, but those rules should remain explainable to users.

Of these goals, I consider transparency and access to reliable information the most important. A search engine cannot be perfectly neutral, but users should be able to distinguish advertisements from search results, understand the general basis of ranking, and encounter trustworthy information from multiple relevant sources.
